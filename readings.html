As we may think reading response:

The author talks here about the future of science after the war. He poses the question on whether or not science would have a future in a peace
driven society by bringing an example of the physicists who have been making nuclear bombs all through the war and asks if they would
have any jobs since there would probably be no more jobs in making nukes.

He is concerned that science has been converging too much toward violence and less towards helping people. This essay was written during
the Hiroshima nuclear bombing, which brought up a lot of questions about the destructive nature of science.

He argues in his first section that science has evolved a long way since the first invention of science but our methods on viewing its results
and interpreting them have stayed the same for generations. In this way, one may think that the author is insinuating that human
creativity is the element that has not evolved and on the contrary, it has become more prone to violence. Creativity feeds science and science
feeds creativity. If you are missing one of those there is no way that the scientific method can evolve more then what it is today.

In the second part, he discusses the concept of having all of human writing combined in a small room thanks to microphotography.
Here we may think that he is debating on the idea of the online encyclopedia.

Today we have Wikipedia which everyone can edit and add text on whatever they want such as a specific animal, Sherlock Holmes, cross
correlation, and the history of chocolate. He envisioned a future where we could have something like that thanks to photography, but we realized
his idea with computers and the internet. He also envisioned an algorithm that could search the entirity of human knowledge using a few short
words which would be called the Memex. He was trying to describe the first information search engines which would make information available
to people who do not have access to the library. Nowadays, search engines can even create the information that he was hoping
people could eventually rapidly access.

Tim Berners Lee's The world wide web reading response:

In this article Tim Berners Lee describes the advantages of the World Wide Web over other established web frameworks such as Gopher
and WAIS. The idea of the WWW was that information could be easily linked to another website or page via addresses sent between computers. These
addresses, in turn, could be searched up and displayed in a browser on a computer. By doing this, the web's information could be more easily
accessible and also more interlinked so users wouldn't have to manually search for every individual page. Also, the WWW gives a standard for all
web pages to use, so distribution and creation of information on the web would be more streamlined and easier for everyone to
access. In addition, the ability to transfer rich media such as pictures and files makes HTTP a very easy way to disseminate rich information.

The advantage of the WWW is that it made the access of any information an activity that any user could do without super advanced knowledge
of how computer systems worked. This led to the web being not only an academic thing, but a household thing. It also made the web interesting,
because entertainment, news and data could be accessed at any time with little effort.
